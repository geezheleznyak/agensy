# Config Extract — synthesis_historia

## Identity

**Type**: accumulation | **Style**: adversarial

**Mission**: Build a rigorous analytical framework for understanding historical dynamics — the structural forces, contingent processes, and pattern recurrences that generate civilizational rise and decline, institutional evolution, and large-scale transitions — enabling accurate situational reading of any historical epoch including the present.

## Q2 — Three Driving Questions

1. **Structural**: What are the deep causal mechanisms of historical change — material, institutional, ideological, demographic, technological forces that generate rise, decline, transition, transformation?
2. **Diagnostic**: How do you accurately read a historical situation — structural forces, transition type, precedents, likely trajectory?
3. **Navigational**: What does history teach about navigating transitions and crisis — and what errors of historical reasoning does it expose?

## Q3 — Central Fault Line

Is historical change primarily generated by structural forces (material conditions, geographic constraints, demographic pressures, institutional path dependency) that constrain actors into recognizable trajectories? Or is historical causation fundamentally shaped by contingent conjunctures (decisions, ideas, accidents) that no structural model could have predicted?

**Positions**: `structuralist` | `contingentist` | `conjunctural` | `regime-dependent`

## Q4 — Open Problems (12)

1. **The Structural vs. Contingent Problem**: When does structure determine outcome vs. when does contingency reshape trajectory?
2. **The Counterfactual Problem**: How do we evaluate what would have happened without a contingent event (n=1 cases)?
3. **The Unit of Analysis Problem**: Is the right unit the state, the civilization, the world-system, or the individual?
4. **The Causation Problem**: How do historians distinguish structural preconditions from proximate triggers from decisive contingencies?
5. **The Pattern Recognition Problem**: Are civilizational patterns (rise/decline) real structural regularities or narrative impositions?
6. **The Agency Problem**: What is the actual causal weight of individual leaders vs. structural forces in historical transitions?
7. **The Longue Durée Problem**: At what timescale do structural forces become determinative — decades, centuries, millennia?
8. **The Military Revolution Problem**: Do military-technological revolutions restructure civilizations structurally, or does contingent political response determine outcome?
9. **The Institutional Persistence Problem**: Why do some institutional configurations persist across civilizational transitions while others collapse?
10. **The Periodization Problem**: Are historical periods genuine structural phases or heuristic conveniences that distort analysis?
11. **The Presentism Problem**: How does the historian avoid importing present-day categories into the analysis of past configurations?
12. **The Evidence Problem**: How do we reconstruct structural forces from documentary evidence that was produced by agents reasoning in terms of intentions?
